# Crossrail retrospective drift analysis

**Case repository:** [`crossrail-retrospective-2026`](https://github.com/antnewman/crossrail-retrospective-2026)
**Status:** In progress (April 2026).
**Supabase schema:** `crossrail_retrospective`.
**Anchor:** *Crossrail Business Case Update Summary Report* (July 2011), plus subsequent National Audit Office reports (2014, 2019, 2021) and Committee of Public Accounts reports (2014, 2021) that tracked progress against the 2011 baselines. This case is a retrospective validation of published assumptions rather than an operationalisation of a single parliamentary recommendation.

## Question

Thirteen years after the 2011 baseline, do the original Crossrail business case assumptions hold when scored against outturn data? The original commitments were a £14.8bn funding envelope (2010-11 prices), a transport-only benefit-cost ratio of 1.97, and approximately 200 million annual passenger journeys at full opening. The Elizabeth Line has been open since May 2022 and is now in its fourth year of operation.

## Assumptions under investigation

1. Construction funding envelope: £14.8bn (2010-11 prices). *Primary.*
2. Annual passenger journeys at full opening: approximately 200 million.
3. Annual Elizabeth Line passenger revenue, 2024-25: £1.037bn (December 2019 TfL Business Plan forecast).
4. Benefit-cost ratio, DfT value (transport only): 1.97.
5. Value-for-money band, DfT classification: "medium" (1.5 to 2.0).

## External series used

ONS Construction Output Price Index (Infrastructure new work), BCIS Tender Price Index (for caveat 1 comparison only), ORR Elizabeth Line passenger journeys (annual and quarterly), TfL Elizabeth Line passenger income, ONS GDP deflator, Bank of England Bank Rate.

## Mandatory caveats

1. Construction cost index choice: ONS Construction OPI (Infrastructure) is the free public reprice basis; the canonical BCIS Tender Price Index is subscription-restricted and referenced only where a public comparison point is available. The direction of the finding is robust to the index choice; the magnitude is not.
2. Pandemic as a break in the demand series: the 2011 forecast did not contemplate a pandemic; the analysis separates pre-pandemic trend drift from post-pandemic recovery drift, so that variance is not mis-attributed.
3. Multiple restated business cases: the 2011 business case was informally updated as funding increases were announced, but no comprehensive restated BCR with full methodology was ever published. The comparison is the 2011 baseline against 2024-25 outturn, not a sequence of restated BCRs.
